Remote Vehicle Assistance at Motional

In October 2023, I was promoted and switched teams to lead the design of Remote Vehicle Assistance (RVA) at Motional. The software we designed enabled remote operators to overcome challenging AV scenarios with dynamic tools, removing the need for human intervention from the driver's seat. Our main users were these remote operators, otherwise known as agents. The agents worked from duel monitor workstations located in the Command Center room of our Las Vegas office.

The Team

The team dynamic on RVA was a bit intense when I first joined. Everyone was in a constant rush to meet harsh deadlines, but there were limited team members, which caused stress on everyone. The stress led to difficult and sometimes heated conversations, which resulted in more stress. We slowly added more team members and worked out a process that better suited the team. Our team worked in a rapid-paced, agile process where each project was broken down into small and manageable chunks, only focusing on phase 0 (P0) to start. For phase 0, we built the minimum viable product (MVP), the minimum features needed for the feature to be usable by our agents. This allowed us to turn projects around quickly, engineering to build them in a sprint, and research and design to run usability testing, finding improvements we need to make for the next phase.



Once the team got used to the process and could distribute the workload, I worked on enhancing the relationships on the team. I promoted stronger collaboration, communication, and efficiency by organizing team-building exercises, encouraging open communication, and focusing on clear and concise language when conversing with team members. After the team adapted to more efficient communication styles, we were able to have calm conversations even when times felt stressful. In the end, we loved working together as a team and felt we were able to overcome any challenges we faced.

Onboarding to RVA

When I first transitioned to the RVA team, there wasn't much of an onboarding process for new team members. While coming up to speed and piecing information together, I created a new onboarding process document in Figma (shown below on the left) breaking down everything I used to understand the team. I then used this to bring my direct report up to speed only a month after I joined. This effort cut onboarding new team members' time in half and was also useful for other teams to gain insight into how our team functioned.

Organization

In addition to improving the onboarding process, I also enhanced our team organization. I joined RVA four months after the previous and only designer had been laid off, and due to this, had no one to guide me through the Figma Files. Unfortunately, the files I inherited were bare bones, lacking any key dates, description, or useful information, making the files unusable. This was a turning point where I realized the importance of clear and extensive documentation.

In turn, I made it my mission to create structured Figma templates (pictured below) that anyone could dive into and understand exactly what was intended at each step of the process. In the template, the first page gives an overview of project information and subsequent pages break down each step of our process. This template kept our design handoffs consistent as well, making it quicker and easier for engineering to digest each new feature. Key dates, points of contact, decisions made, open questions, risks and more are now well documented throughout all our project documents.

Prior to my joining, there had been no design system or reusable components. I created a design system (shown on the right above) for the RVA team and built a set of complex components. The complex components allowed designers to produce screens and prototypes in half the time, and made changing instances easy with the flip of a toggle.

By creating templates for our design process in Figma and a comprehensive design system, I streamlined our workflows and ensured clarity through detailed documentation. As our team grew, we were able to stay efficient, consistent and organized.

Hybrid Working

Since we had offices worldwide, our team spent the majority of our time together virtually. We were efficient at finding ways to work together using various remote tools and often prioritized asynchronous communication. This meant we employed strong skills at refining content and clearly explaining our needs and questions to other team members. We often collaborated in Google Docs, Miro boards, and Figma files, leaving comments, questions, and critiques.

We collaborated across timezones with team members working out of Boston, LA, Singapore, Pittsburgh, and Chicago. The time difference ranged from 1-3 hours to 12 hours. A few times a year, we would visit Las Vegas, the center of all operations, for in-person research sessions, hands on learning, and to sync face to face.

Our designers and researchers mostly worked out of the Boston office, so we would get together in person for team whiteboarding sessions. At one point, my direct report was located in LA while I remained in Boston. We used this 3 hour time difference to our benefit by collaborating asynchronously on features and gaining 3 additional working hours per day for our shared projects.

Additional Process Improvements

Feasibility Checks

One problem we continuously faced on RVA was running into feasibility issues. We would learn about the constraints after we handed off our designs. This would prolong the project, lead to design rework, and postpone engineeringâ€™s start on the effort. To solve this problem, I implemented feasibility checks at multiple steps of our process. After the product team synced on the requirements and brainstormed as a team, we would meet with engineering to ask our open questions and give them a rough idea of potential solutions. Engineering was able to inform us what was possible, or workarounds we could make. We also had a second feasibility check right after concept testing and before we finalized the designs. This reduced rework on the design side and gave engineering a sense of what to expect with the final handoff. We were also able to incorporate feedback and requests from engineering, making it a full team collaborative process.


Design Needs Checklist

Another issue we encountered when I first joined the team was a misunderstanding of project requirements and expectations from PM to research and design. The misunderstanding was due to requirements being written for both engineering and design. To solve this, I set up meetings with the product team members to read through the requirements together, gain a shared understanding, and specifically list out the expectations from design at each phase of the project. Design was then able to use this as a reference throughout the project and it aligned the team on future engagements.
